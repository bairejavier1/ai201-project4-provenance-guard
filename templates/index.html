Understanding your score

What does the percentage mean?

Every submission produces a combined score from 0% to 100%. This number reflects how strongly the two detection signals believe the content was generated by an AI writing tool. A higher percentage means more AI-like patterns were found. A lower percentage means the writing looks more like a human wrote it.

The score is not a verdict — it is a confidence estimate. The system is designed to be cautious: it requires a score of 65% or higher before labeling content as AI-generated, because falsely accusing a human creator is considered worse than missing an AI submission.

Score ranges at a glance

65% – 100% — ⚠️ Likely AI-Generated
Both signals agree the writing shows strong AI patterns — uniform sentence lengths, formulaic phrasing, hedged vagueness, and low vocabulary variation.
36% – 64% — 🔍 Origin Unclear
The signals are mixed or weak. The system cannot confidently classify the content either way. This is intentional — an honest uncertain result is better than a confident wrong one.
0% – 35% — ✅ Likely Human-Written
The writing shows strong human patterns — variable sentence lengths, rich vocabulary, natural punctuation, and an absence of formulaic AI transitions.

The two signals explained

Signal 1
LLM Semantic Analysis
Sends your text to a large language model (Groq llama-3.3-70b) and asks it to assess whether the writing reads as AI-generated. It looks at meaning, style, and phrasing — things like overuse of hedging words ("various", "numerous"), perfectly balanced arguments, and absence of personal detail. This signal understands what is being said.
Signal 2
Stylometric Heuristics
Runs entirely in Python with no API call. Measures four statistical properties of the text's structure — sentence-length variance, vocabulary diversity (type-token ratio), punctuation density, and frequency of formulaic transition phrases. AI writing tends to be more statistically uniform than human writing. This signal looks at how something is written, not what it says.

Why the two signals sometimes disagree

The LLM signal and the stylometric signal measure completely different things. It is possible for them to point in opposite directions — for example, a formal academic essay might have uniform sentence lengths (high stylometric score) but genuine personal insight that the LLM recognises as human (low LLM score).

When the two signals disagree by more than 30 percentage points, the system applies a disagreement penalty — the combined score is capped at 70% regardless of the arithmetic result. This prevents the system from being confidently wrong. A large disagreement between signals is itself evidence of uncertainty.

When scores are less reliable

The system will show a yellow warning banner when any of these conditions apply:

⚠
Short text (under 50 words) — the stylometric signal needs multiple sentences to compute meaningful statistics. Very short texts may score as uncertain regardless of their true origin.
⚠
Non-Latin script — the stylometric heuristics were tuned on English text and produce unreliable scores for Arabic, Chinese, Japanese, and other scripts. Only the LLM signal is active.
⚠
Source code — braces, indentation, and semicolons make the structural analysis meaningless for programming languages. Only the LLM signal is active.
⚠
Long text (over 4,000 characters) — the LLM only analyzes the first 4,000 characters. The warning tells you what percentage of the text was covered semantically. The stylometric signal always analyzes the full text.

If you believe the score is wrong

No AI detection system is perfect. If your content is labeled as AI-generated but you wrote it yourself, you can submit an appeal directly from the result card. Your explanation is logged alongside the original classification and queued for human review. The status changes to "under review" immediately — the classification is no longer treated as final.

Common reasons scores may be misleading: writing in a formal academic style, being a non-native English speaker, writing in a genre with structured conventions (legal, technical, or business writing), or submitting very short creative work. All of these are worth mentioning in your appeal reasoning.
